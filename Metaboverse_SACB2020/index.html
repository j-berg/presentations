Gazing into the Metaboverse

Metabolic network pattern recognition tools contextualize multi-omic data and reveal disease-relevant signatures

Jordan A. Berg, Youjia Zhou, Yeyun Ouyang, T. Cameron Waller, Sara M. Nowinski, Tyler Van Ry, Ian George, James E. Cox, Bei Wang, Jared Rutter

Abstract

Due to the scale and complexity inherent in metabolism, systems approaches that are easily accessible to the general researcher are necessary. We developed Metaboverse, a user-friendly, interactive, and cross-platform analysis and visualization tool for data layered on metabolic networks. Metaboverse offers the ability to integrate multi-omic, multi-condition, time course, and metabolomics tracing datasets on over ninety model organisms. We developed a search engine that comprehensively searches and analyzes the network for complex regulatory patterns within the sequencing, proteomics, and metabolomics data, integrating enzyme information into the metabolic network to create a robust search space. This framework will be foundational in allowing users to analyze their data in a more holistic manner that integrates all measured information of their biological system to rapidly identify interesting regulatory patterns within the global metabolic network. Metaboverse is available under a GPL-3.0 open-source license and a beta version can be found at https://github.com/Metaboverse/Metaboverse.

Innovations

Automated reaction pattern searches

We developed an algorithm that automates the discovery of reaction patterns across the entire metabolic network based on the user-provided omics data. Metaboverse identifies a variety of different patterns. For example, the "Average" reaction pattern compares the averages of the inputs against the averages of the outputs of a reaction.

Handling data sparsity in metabolomics

Metabolomics datasets often, if not always, only quantify a portion of the total metabolites involved in human metabolism and the metabolism of other organisms. This leads to gaps in the metabolic network and complicates pattern analysis within the metabolic network. Thus, we developed a reaction collapsing algorithm that bridges reactions with missing values and enables reaction pattern searching with missing values.

Metaboverse Overview

Metaboverse is packaged with a user-friendly interactive interface where data and metadata are provided and the data are curated onto the metabolic network.

An illustration outlining the data input, processing, and exploration steps of the Metaboverse package. The user provides the name of the organism of interest from a drop-down menu along with an output location. The user then has the option to provide transcriptomics, proteomics, and/or metabolomics datasets. These datasets can be single- or multi-condition or time-course experiments. Data is formatted as follows: row names are the measured entity names or IDs, the first column is a log2(fold change) or other measurement value, and the second column is a statistical measurement. For time-course and multi-condition datasets, this pattern is repeated for each subsequent sample. During this step, the user can also provide sample labels and other modifiers to customize the curation and display of the data on the curated reaction network. Metaboverse will then build the model. Once the model is complete, the user will be able to visualize the patterns identified within reactions, explore pathway-specific or general perturbation networks, and perform general pathway and nearest reaction neighborhood exploration of the data.

Identifying canonical and novel metabolic patterns in lung adenocarcinomas

Lung cancer remains a leading cause of death worldwide, and an improved understanding of the metabolism of these tumors is essential in understanding how to treat this disease more effectively. We were particularly interested in studying a dataset we recently re-analyzed as we had manually identified interesting reaction patterns that were not identified in the original study.

Identification of xanthine regulation by both the pattern recognition and perturbation analysis modules

Xantine, and other nucleotide metabolism components, were found to be significantly regulated in this study.

Identification of a novel regulation event in lung adenocarcinomas

As we previously emphasized, by approaching these perturbations in a reaction-centric approach, we can identify regulatory behavior that further contextualizes the data. For example, using a more manual approach, we previously highlighted the modest upregulation of glyceric acid coupled with the proximal downregulation of 3-Phosphoglyceric acid which could indicate the decreased activity of glycerate kinase. This connection was missed in the original study but was quickly and automatically highlighted by Metaboverse's reaction pattern analysis module. This sub-pathway activity is consequential as it has connections to serine metabolism which contributes to generating an ideal environment for tumorigenesis.

Identification of a enzymatic regulatory event that corresponds with poor prognosis

Consistent with the original study and our recent re-analysis of the data, nucleotide metabolism was broadly upregulated in adenocarcinomas and was highlighted in the reaction pattern and perturbation network analyses in Metaboverse. In particular, a top-scoring reaction pattern identified by Metaboverse involved the transformation ofdc-adenosyl methionine and spermidine to form spermine and 5′-methylthioadenosine by Spermine Synthase (SMS; top image). Although this reaction is a step in polyamine synthesis pathway, it is also connected with nucleotide metabolism. Individual components related to these metabolites were identified and discussed in the original study in the context of the polyamine synthesis pathway. Additionally, in our previous re-analysis of the dataset, we pointed to a potential regulatory action stemming from SpermidineSynthase (SRM) using manual methods of analysis. However, this specific reaction pattern identified automatically byMetaboverse implicated a regulatory role for Spermine Synthase in this patient cohort that was not identified in the original study or our re-analysis. We can further explore this hypothesis using tools available within the public database, The Protein Expression Atlas [39–41] (v19.3), to explore the clinical implications of this observed pattern. By analyzing survival rates of a lung adenocarcinoma (LUAD) cohort from The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA), we discovered a striking relationship in SMS gene expression levels and 5-year survival (bottom image), where, at a fragments-per-kilobase-million (FPKM) cut-off of 50, 5-year survival in the SMS-high expression group is only 17% (n=104) versus 47% in the SMS-low expression group (n=396) (log-rank Kaplain-Meier p-value = 1.6E-05). Thus, this regulatory pattern in LUAD patient scorresponds to an increase in SMS activity and poor prognoses.
